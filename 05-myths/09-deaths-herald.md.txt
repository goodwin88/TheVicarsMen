Death's Herald

I.

Huginn stuck his black beak
into sleeping Odin’s ear
and whispered,

“We know of worlds beyond worlds
and of ages long past into dust;
of places and times
without gods, nor mortals,
nor form, nor light.”

Odin shifted uncomfortably in his sleep.
He brushed the bird away
and rolled to one side.

Oily black Muninn nuzzled close
to Odin’s other ear and sighed,

“We remember all this and more.
We have seen all futures,
beyond Ragnarok, beyond time,
to the end of all ending -
and you are not there.”

Odin frowned, still sleeping.
His dreams were now tainted and disturbed.
He swatted the crows away
and turned once more.

Drawing his furs close,
pelts piled high against the cold
and the nattering of bothersome birds.
Nestled, he slept.


II.

He fell into a sleep so deep
that it was close to death,
so similar
that Death came calling.

“You crows, where is your master?
I may have business with him.”

Huginn and Muninn perched
upon the piled hides.
It was a mounding hill of soft plush skins.
They scratched and cackled.

“He buries himself away.”
“He sleeps now without us.”
“And without us, he will go mad.”

Death saw that this was true.
The All-father lay curled as a babe
beneath his covers.
A brooding gem in the heart of a mountain.

Death said,
“I cannot reach him this way.”
And the crows laughed wickedly.

“No! Of course you cannot!”
“There is no reaching him now.”
“Not until he wakes, if he wakes.”
“He will not hear us; what is he dreaming?”
“What visions?”

Death was unused to being denied.
This mindless sleep was troubling.
“I must wake him, if I can.
I must know the truth.”

The birds flew to Death
and sidled close.
They rustled inky feathers
and spoke in cloying tones.

“Must know the truth?”
“We know many truths.”
“Would you know them all?”

Death kicked at them
and they squawked into the air,
disturbed.
“Get off me,” Death said.

It was then that the mountain
of furs shuddered;
Odin was stirring.

“He lives!” Death said.
“He is dying,” said Huginn.
“He dreams without us,
and without end,” said Muninn.

The three of them fell quiet,
glum and moved to pity
by the moaning
deep below.


III.

Loki had been watching all along
as a flea within the furs.
He leaped up and revealed himself.
He said,

“Odin is not lost to us.
Come to Mimir’s well.
His eye is there,
down in the waters.

The well is a place of wisdom.
His eye is there
at the bottom,
so that he might see all."

And so they went,
with Death astride the trickster
now a mare, glossy and
rippling with muscle.

Death’s hands tangled
into Loki’s mane and
Death whispered close
to his tender horse ear,

“You work between my legs,
and I know your reputation.
Beware, I will have you one day
when your time has come.”

Loki swore to seek no mischief,
and his hair streaked white
in Death’s grip.

Steed and rider arrived
to find the crows already
crouched around the well.
They all gathered close to peer within.

“We see it.”
“There in the deep.”
“But we cannot fly to it.”
Death pointed to Loki,

“Become a fish and fetch us the eye.”

Loki became a sleek salmon.
He flashed through the air,
diving with a wet plop
down, down into the water.


IV.

After some time they began to worry.
Was this treachery,
or misfortune?
Death saw a mouse and had an idea.

“Mouse, drown yourself in the well
so I may come to collect you at the bottom.”
The mouse trembled with fear,
but he shook his head, saying,

“I cannot; my family waits for me.
We are small and far from home.
I must return to protect them.”

Death was not to be denied twice,
“You are terrified
and will soon die from fright;
I will have you either way.

Die in the well,
so that I may enter.
Do this for me and
your family will mourn a hero.”

“Yes,” said Huginn,
crowding close to the mouse.
“Yes,” said Muninn,
doing much the same.

“We know this to be true;
you will do your kind
a great service.”

The mouse’s heart beat painfully fast.
Death promised,
“You will not suffer.”

The small mouse nodded dumbly.
He was just a mouse after all.
He did not struggle when the
crows flew him over the well

And laughing,
they dropped him in with
a wet plop.


V.

The mouse could not swim.
He clawed at the water uselessly,
seeing Death and the birds looking
down at him, waiting and watching.

They receded up and away,
and he felt nothing but
the cold water all around,
until it was completely dark.

He panicked and bubbled out his breath.
Tumbling and sinking and falling,
he trailed blood and bubbles.
And still further down he went.

Strange creatures brushed his legs;
serpents with needle-sharp
teeth like glass.

He thought of his family;
his sons were still infants,
his wife again pregnant.
How would they know what happened?

He gulped out his last,
and Death joined him.
Death held his hand
and they continued down together.

The mouse was now unafraid.
Smoothly they dropped
through dark water
like two stars falling.


VI.

Later, they saw Odin’s forsaken eye,
small and distant below.
They saw the eye left behind
as the price for knowledge.

As they drifted to it,
Death saw Loki wriggling desperately.
He had tried to betray them,
to take the eye for himself.

“Death, help me! I am blind!”

Death did not answer.

“I plucked my eye to take his own,
but he is mad - what I saw was horrible.
I cast it from me, but I could still see!
I plucked my other, but I could still see!”

Loki churned and roiled,
streaming blood and bubbles.
He cried and wailed
and worked his mouth.

Death and the mouse settled lightly
to the bottom of the well.
The eye was there, clouded,
softly swollen and discarded.

Together they held it,
and Death whispered close,
“You are dreaming,
you are dreaming.”

The unblinking eye saw everything.
Odin was not dead, only sleeping.
Odin gasped; he shrugged off his covers,
and awoke.
