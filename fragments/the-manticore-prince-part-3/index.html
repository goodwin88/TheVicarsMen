    The Manticore Prince (Part 3)

    The puzzle to Folimari's Cube was almost complete, and yet

    Sam clutched at Helmson's charred remains, kneeling with them in his arms and sobbing, shaking with grief. There was no other sound, beyond his cries. All had paused to watch in stunned silence, even the air was still. He rocked back and forth with the smoking bones, smudging his cheek to them and sniffling low incoherent mumbles.

    In the middle of the scorched playing field, this tableau held for minutes, seemingly drawn out into hours, days even, before Folimari breathlessly raced across the pitch, pushing past Joyce. The old Dreamer dropped next to Sam and struggled to pull him away from the body.

    Joyce broke from his stupor and whimpered pathetically, "It's not my fault. I didn't know-"

    He turned to any who might face him, seeking recognition, some form of forgiveness and escape from this smothering shame. No one met his gaze, he felt as though he were drowning.

    "I didn't know. I'm sorry."

    Slowly, the crowd began to rustle with whispers, and there rang out a hiss, then a boo of disapproval. Useless betting vouchers flew and fluttered from all about the stadium.

    Soon others arrived to the field, medics, police, even some members of the council. Joyce was roughly pulled aside, and he didn't resist; his head hung limply to his chest.

    As he was ushered past, Sam looked up through teary eyes, and his face contorted with a dramatic, profound awfulness from sorrow into a blind and bloody rage.

    He spit, struggling to get up as Folimari restrained him.

    "You!" His raw swollen eyes bulged with emotion, and he shrugged off Folimari as though shedding his skin, emerging larger somehow. He dropped dead Helmson to the ground and rose as a dark wave cresting, taller and bonier than ever, his hair wildly writhing like some soft sea creature. He lurched a step forward, then another, breaking into a run after Joyce.

    "You!" He howled again, his mouth hanging open, his teeth flashing.

    One of the escorting officers turned with raised hands.

    "Sir, stay back and let us han-"

    But Sam's face opened six different ways into lively wet matter that flowed around the policeman, pulling him apart as easily as runny eggs. Sam did not slow his charge, scooping up and through the obstacle. Sam's flesh bloomed about in wild arcs, consuming and erupting with the urgent, crashing violence of a waterfall. From his skin, slavering wolves surfaced then submerged, followed by a thousand tentacles wielding a thousand crude blades slicing the air, and then hands upon hands reached for Joyce with knuckles sprouting fingers sprouting talons sprouting scorpion stingers and fish hooks on fire.

    He sheared through the other officer with an exaggerated gory splatter, like a special effect done with buckets and an excess of pig's blood. Sam had now spiraled into a maelstrom of flesh and destruction, spewing hatchets and missile swarms and volcanic geysers of acidic radioactive piranha harpies, lightning in all directions.

    There was chaos in the stands. Sam rampaged for several days, all-consuming and unstoppable.

    Helmson clutched at Sam's charred remains, kneeling with them in his arms and sobbing, shaking with grief. Again the coliseum heavy with silence, again comes Folimari knocking Joyce aside.

    Helmson looks to the old Dreamer, his face broken with pain and sorrow.

    "My friend, my sweet Sam. Sweet child, Sam."

    Folimari knelt and embraced both him and the body.

    At some point Joyce scuffled by in the custody two legionnaires. Helmson struggled impotently in Folimari's arms, yelling after him, "You! It should have been you - not him! Not my Sam! Folimari, let me go!"

    "We have him, Helmson. He will stand for his crimes."

    "No, it is not enough! It's not enough!"

    And as it turned out, it wasn't. At the trial, his defense attorney made the case that Joyce was not mentally fit to be held to full account for his actions. He was said to have suffered considerable brain damage due to working so intimately with his creations, which were revealed to be toxic.

    Convicted of some lesser form of negligent manslaughter, Joyce was remanded to therapeutic custody for treatment and further evaluation. The case was closed, Sam had no living family to appeal. The remaining manticores were destroyed, the war continued and Helmson spent the rest of his days frequently visiting Sam's grave and telling him the latest salon gossip. At some point in his old age, Helmson commits suicide on New Year's Eve.

    Sam spilled over out of the arena into streets lined with the most experienced and martial Dreamers, they intended to try and contain him. He was now monstrously huge, gouging craters of asphalt into his mouths and pulling down the masonry and steel from the buildings as he passed. He was still thrashing in full tantrum, and all that he touched seemed only to feed his anger and strength. He spread like wildfire, reducing the city to rubble from one block to the next. Nothing they did seemed to have any lasting effect as he simply absorbed or negated anything directed to him.

    It was only when it seemed that he might reach the Library did the council realize just how serious this was - not just a ruined neighborhood, not just a natural disaster, Sam had become a vast singularity of wanton calamity. Should he reach the Library, it was not clear what would happen. It was a gateway, as well as a repository; would he pass through it? And if so to where? And if he added to his being all that the Library had to offer, he would certainly become as a god, churning as Azathoth in the void.

    The Dreamers were not quite ready see that narrative through.

    Convicted of some lesser form of negligent manslaughter, Joyce was remanded to therapeutic custody for treatment and further evaluation.

    Years later Helmson began to visit him regularly, apparently at peace with the past and gaining the trust of the staff until one day, Helmson swipes a sharp pencil from a lax attendant and repeatedly stabs Joyce in the neck, claiming vengeance through clenched teeth and spittle.

    Years later Helmson visited him, having reached some inner peace over the situation and still feeling responsible for Joyce in a faintly paternalistic way. He said that it was too much to lose one friend, and that he was not willing to lose another.

    Years later Joyce was delivered a noticed that Helmson had died, succumbing to brain disease brought about by his brief exposure so long ago.

    Years later Helmson goes to visit Joyce and is informed that he has passed away, by complete chance and accident, he is told.

    "I give up, Joy. I can't see a way to solve this. And we're almost out of time, the duel is soon." Helmson let the black cube tumble between them onto the table.

    Joyce stirred sugar into his coffee with grim solemnity, "He's just trying to intimidate me."

    "And you aren't intimidated? These all seem like perfectly credible outcomes to me." Helmson shifted uneasily in his seat, "And I have to say I'm not happy with how many of them see me dead. You're the one who challenged him."

    Joyce picked up the puzzle box and turned it about in his hands. He twisted one side, and then another at random, "There has to be a solution in which Folimari loses, or at least suffers. He's barely affected in any of these, so far. Do you think he rigged this thing? Are these all preset outcomes?"

    Helmson shook his head, "No, that would be crass. Folimari would not load the dice, as it were. He's too much of an egotist to cheat. That's what worries me; I don't particularly enjoy unhappy endings."

    "His Dreaming is so complex. I wasn't expecting it to take this form."

    "What? You thought just because you tinker so literally, that he would follow suit? Joy, he deals in concepts and abstractions, Folimari's Dreams are highly complicated existential metaphors. Truthfully, as clever as you are about your business - he outclasses you. I was frankly surprised that you challenged him; he was right about your creations, you know."

    "But I thought all Dreams had form. There's a war going on right now, a real bloody conflict against a tangible enemy, how else should we fight it?"

    Helmson, shook his head and reached for the cube, taking it.

    "I don't pretend to understand how it works fully, I'm not as metaphysically capable as some. If anything, I'm a literal thinker like you. I make things, practical things. Like this."

    Helmson pulled from inside his pocket what looked to be a large magnifying glass, though it was pierced through the center of the lens with a rotating dial that bore several smaller lenses of different colors, some stacked within stout metal cylinders. He held it up to Joyce and looked through it with one eye, now suddenly huge and blinking.

    Helmson adjusted the device and squinted at the target.

    Within Helmson, the Vicar's Black Spy peered out from one iris, then the other, observing and recording and transmitting. As the night fell, shadows lengthened and deepened. It would be able to move about much more freely at night, though the inky, petroleum black of the cube still registered high on its list of optimal havens. The spy saw the visions inside it, both first hand and through Helmson's eyes. The box was extremely prescient, more sensitive to narrative than perhaps even its creator could understand. It had picked up signals from beyond its realm, but the clues were lost on Helmson. Only Folimari himself might recognize the extra material, additions to his creation that he did not intend.

    The Vicar would be pleased at the information, the open evidence. He does not deal in obfuscation, all his plans are bare and discernable. Indeed, such is his goal for all things: to be plainly understood. Alexandria would be struck down to smoking rubble, the heart of their power fallen even as the Dreamers overwhelmed his martial forces elsewhere along the front line.

There is a box that shows you possible futures. Under closer scrutiny it reveals itself to be a metaphor, using one story to tell another.
It is a receiver, like a radio. And it picks up imagery from other sources near it on the dial, other stories.
There actually is a threat of attack, but it comes unregarded in another narrative altogether.
The box suggests this openly, but it is unrecognized as such.
The box points to the narrative as metaphor as metaphor.
The outcome to all of this should come as no surprise, and it will be seen as obvious in retrospect. It was there all along. Only the Dreamers play with masks and subterfuge.
The Vicar is concerned with bare truth and ruthless revelation.
His agents of destruction are exactly as they appear; what reads as distressing is due to distress. It is that simple. It is not clever, it is not poetry.
There is terror in the genuine, raw open communication of being. Not only because revealing yourself makes you vulnerable (and that is frightening for you), but also because what is revealed, unvarnished and ungilded, is the awful bedrock of existence, without niceties or artifice (and that is frightening for others).

    The spies, both Black and White, piece this together from their joined information. They send this back to their superior. They themselves are watched by the Gray spy and she takes great pleasure in their flitting espionage. She reports to another. Black and White exist as imperfect echoes of her being. The Vicar is not God, and his spies are copies she recognizes as attempts to reproduce herself. She is one of the few females in this story, and she appears here near the end, because this is a tangled world of words and flawed men; other points of view would not be believed. She turns to you as a Greek chorus, and she tells you most directly:

    "This one will kill himself; he is only waiting for the right alignment of circumstance, opportunity, and necessity."

    She looks back to the narrative, watching the spies who watch for the Vicar, the eventual loser of his war. Black watches from within Helmson's eyes. White watches from the glaring lights of Joyce's studio. Helmson watches Joyce with pity and envy. His life will never be as exciting nor as dangerous as the younger man's. Joyce watches Helmson with creeping anxiety and disgust. The older man is weak and unaccomplished, without skill or imagination. Helmson will be unable to help him make sense of what threatens and dooms him. He is no adequate mentor, though that is his role. Others watch others, with varying degrees of comprehension. The Vicar watches all that can be seen, which is not quite omniscience. His sigil eye remains wide and unblinking.
